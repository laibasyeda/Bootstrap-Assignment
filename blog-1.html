The virus of the month: Rhinovirus


Today we are highlighting perhaps the most inconspicuous and well known guilty party of the normal cold: Rhinovirus. Rhinovirus: Causing most upper respiratory infections, this bug prefers the cold, which is why the cold virus remains so prevalent in the fall and winter. This virus is very difficult and can be easily spread by sneezing, coughing and even touching contaminated surfaces. Rhinovirus infections usually cause mild symptoms, such as a runny nose, sore throat, and cough, but they can be more severe in people with asthma or weakened immune systems. While it seems to be a fact of life and is an annoyance to say the least, the better news is most people recover in just a few days with rest, hydration, and over the counter solutions. I was reminded as we transition another month into a different season of what it can be like to be an adult with ADHD trying to regulatoryange patterns.



Prevalence:
The majority of cases of the common cold are due to rhinovirus, the most prevalent of the upper respiratory infections.

Seasonality:
Infection is highest in the colder months of fall and winter because the virus likes lower temperatures.

Transmission:
The novel coronavirus (COVID-19) is a highly contagious disease that spreads by respiratory droplets emitted when you sneezing cough, or can be transmitted by touch after contact with a contaminated surface.

Symptoms:
These symptoms are usually associated with a runny nose, sore throats, coughing, sneezing, congestion, headaches, and a low fever.

Risk Groups:
Likewise, COVID-19 can have more serious outcomes in those with asthma, chronic respiratory conditions, or weakened immune systems.


Good strategies such as hand washing in combination with coughing and sneezing into tissues, or tissues made out of your elbow, and for contact with infected people is Public health measure(properties).

Key Takeaways:
There is no cure; treatment is directed toward symptom relief -- rest, hydration, over-the-counter.
